# The BETTR project: Bayesian Evaluation of Trauma Treatments for Refugees 

A key goal of clinical psychological science is to improve treatment options for individuals experiencing mental health problems associated with trauma, loss, illness, and other adversity. Treatment options have improved substantially in recent decades, as reflected in the development of evidence-based guidelines. However, challenges remain! 
First, certain (often smaller) patient groups are underrepresented in research. This includes individuals with co-occurring conditions (e.g., posttraumatic stress disorder and depression), multiple loss and trauma, systemic difficulties, and a combination of mental health issues and psychosocial difficulties. Refugees from war-affected regions are one example. As a result, it is often unclear what treatment interventions are most effective for these more vulnerable groups. 
Second, innovative (trauma-focused) interventions tailored to these groups are increasingly being developed and implemented, for instance, at ARQ National Psychotrauma Centre. Yet, many of these interventions have not been systematically evaluated, limiting our ability to determine whether, how, and for whom they work. 

These two challenges are closely intertwined and are compounded by a practical constraint: large-scale randomized controlled trials—the current gold standard—are often not feasible in clinical settings, particularly for smaller and especially vulnerable patient groups. 
The BETTR project addresses this gap by developing and applying innovative methodological and statistical approaches that enable the rigorous evaluation of existing and new interventions in small, specific patient groups, and by providing clinical researchers with easily applicable tools to evaluate their treatments for clinical and research purposes.

### Bayesian and Information-Theoretic Evaluation of (Informative) Hypotheses 

Traditionally, frequentist approaches typically test a null hypothesis (e.g., “no treatment effect”). While useful, clinical researchers who evaluate treatment interventions often have theory-based expectations about what works, for whom, and in which direction. 
Model selection methods are well suited to evaluate theory-driven hypotheses. They allow us to calculate different model comparison metrics (e.g., Bayes factors) to assess the amount of evidence for specific, competing, informative hypotheses – based on data obtained during one or more treatments. 

Within the BETTR project, Bayesian and Information-Theoretic approaches are used complementarily to provide accessible tools for evaluating interventions in small and specific patient groups, leading to informative and practice-relevant conclusions. 

### Goals of the BETTR project 

* Develop and refine treatment interventions for traumatized refugees and other groups underrepresented in trauma research. 
* Evaluate the effects of these interventions in SCEDs (single case experimental designs) and other small-scale (N < 20) studies. 
* Refine, apply, and share Bayesian and Information-Theoretic methods to facilitate and simplify treatment evaluations and assessment of mediators and moderators of treatment effects. 
* Create accessible statistical tools for clinicians, researchers, and clinical researchers to test the effects of their interventions – both in clinical and research settings.
